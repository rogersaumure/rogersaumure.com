Research Overview

I am an applied behavioral researcher interested in the intersection of consumer psychology and technology. My work examines how generative AI, social media, and translation tools reshape consumer expression, self-disclosure, and behavior — with an eye toward developing insights that are both theoretically grounded and practically useful for the marketers, designers, and policymakers building these systems.

My research addresses three central questions. First, when large language models write alongside us, what happens to our voice? In work with Robert Meyer, I examine how LLM suggestions alter the way consumers express themselves online — and what they accept, reject, or compromise on. Second, when consumers publish goal progress on social media, how does that affect goal pursuit? With Marissa Sharif, I explore the unintended consequences of broadcasting personal milestones. Third, can large language models be deployed to support healthy behavior change at scale? In a separate line of work with Marissa Sharif, I investigate whether AI can meaningfully move the needle on real-world health outcomes.

Methodologically, I rely on unstructured data analysis, multilevel modeling, structural equation modeling, and field experiments. My research has been supported by the Mack Institute for Innovation Management, the Wharton AI and Analytics Initiative, the Center for Health Incentives and Behavioral Economics, and the Puri-Bharadwaj PhD Research Fund. I was honored to receive the 2025 J. Frank Yates Memorial Award from the Society for Judgment and Decision-Making.

Before Wharton, I earned a B.S. in Neuroscience (magna cum laude) at the University of Miami, with minors in Chemistry and Economics.
